# The Lambda landscape: where the weight on social cohesion can come from

The cohesion weight Lambda in the model is the relative importance of the social dimension in wellbeing. The lead paper carries the thesis value 0.876 from the OECD Better Life Index dimension rankings; this document maps every alternative source, with links, and reports what each one would imply for Lambda. Three things this clarifies. First, where the YBLI numbers actually live and what they look like. Second, what the WELLBY literature offers as a stronger alternative, and what its shadow prices imply quantitatively. Third, how the WISE Database and the Stanford GDP-B programme connect to this question, both as data and as methodology.

## Route 1: the OECD Your Better Life Index (YBLI)

This is the source the thesis used and the engine still uses as its default. The interactive tool sits at [oecdbetterlifeindex.org](https://www.oecdbetterlifeindex.org/). Users rate each of the eleven dimensions (housing, income, jobs, community, education, environment, governance, health, life satisfaction, safety, work-life balance) on a 0-to-5 importance scale; the implicit weights are aggregated by country.

Where to download. The OECD's open data is in the Well-being Data Monitor: [oecd.org/en/data/tools/well-being-data-monitor.html](https://www.oecd.org/en/data/tools/well-being-data-monitor.html). The historical BLI series is available in OECD's Data Explorer archive under the dataset code `DF_BLI`: [data-explorer.oecd.org/vis?tenant=archive&df%5Bds%5D=DisseminateArchiveDMZ&df%5Bid%5D=DF_BLI&df%5Bag%5D=OECD](https://data-explorer.oecd.org/vis?tenant=archive&df%5Bds%5D=DisseminateArchiveDMZ&df%5Bid%5D=DF_BLI&df%5Bag%5D=OECD). KAPSARC mirrors the data as CSV at [datasource.kapsarc.org/explore/dataset/oecd-better-life-index](https://datasource.kapsarc.org/explore/dataset/oecd-better-life-index/). There are also community snapshots on Kaggle if a one-click CSV is wanted.

What the data look like. The default weight is 5 on every dimension (so equal weighting). Citizen-rated aggregate weights typically over-weight life satisfaction, health, and education and under-weight environment and civic engagement, with country-level variation that has been documented in OECD methodology notes. For our Lambda, the relevant ratio is the community/support-network weight against the income weight: in the published aggregates this ratio sits around 0.8 to 1.1 depending on country and wave, which is where the 0.876 in the thesis comes from.

The honest statistical caveat (the thing you correctly remembered being uneasy about). The YBLI sample is self-selected: people who came across the website. France had about 16,000 users by 2017 and the count is higher now (the OECD reports more than 130,000 ratings across countries in later waves), but the sample is not population-representative, so the weights are reliable as relative magnitudes between dimensions and weak as absolute population-level point estimates. The underlying dimension scores are admin data and statistically robust; the citizen weights are not.

## Route 2: the WELLBY shadow-price approach (the stronger alternative)

WELLBY stands for one point of life satisfaction on a zero-to-ten scale, for one person, for one year, by analogy with the QALY in health economics. The framework was made operational by Frijters and Krekel in their 2024 *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications* paper, [The WELLBY: a new measure of social value and progress](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-024-03229-5), and it underwrites Layard and De Neve's *Wellbeing: Science and Policy* (Cambridge, 2023). The UK Treasury Green Book wellbeing appraisal guidance (DWP/HM Treasury 2021) is the policy-side counterpart, and it carries shadow prices that translate life-satisfaction points into money: at the central estimate, one WELLBY is roughly £10,000 to £15,000, derived from compensating-variation regressions on UK panel data.

What this offers for Lambda, quantitatively. A standard life-satisfaction regression of the form

LS = a + b · log(income) + d · social_support + controls

returns coefficient ratios that map directly to Lambda. In the most-cited British Household Panel Survey and Understanding Society estimates, b is approximately 0.20 to 0.35 per log income point, and d is approximately 0.40 to 0.70 per point of a 0-10 social-support measure. The implied marginal rate of substitution of social support for log income is roughly 2 to 3.5 to 1, well above the YBLI implicit ratio near 1. The Frijters-Krekel handbook chapter on social relationships specifically (see also [docs.iza.org/dp14447.pdf](https://docs.iza.org/dp14447.pdf) for the related IZA paper on social relationships and life satisfaction) makes this comparison explicit.

What this means for the model. If decision utility is what households maximise and experienced wellbeing is what we judge welfare with, the WELLBY literature is informing the wellbeing side. Adopting the WELLBY shadow prices implies a wellbeing-side Lambda about two to three times higher than the YBLI value, holding the material weight Γ at one. The decoupling and the policy-incidence results are robust to this rescaling, because they rest on the SIGN of the social loss, not its magnitude, but the implied policy gradient (how big a consumption gain a planner would need to compensate for a unit of cohesion loss) is two to three times sharper. This is exactly the kind of quantification a WISE-aligned audience asks for.

The Lambda Estimation Design note in the vault sets out Route 2 as the natural collaboration object: a partner with a representative panel (SOEP, Understanding Society, EU-SILC ad hoc, HRS, GSS, CFPS, ELCA, NIDS, the European Social Survey for cross-country) runs the regression they already run, and we return Lambda in model units plus the policy counterfactual in WELLBYs.

## Route 3: the WISE Database (the curated cross-country alternative)

WISE Horizons is the Snower-Lima de Miranda programme directly: a synthesis of Wellbeing, Inclusion, Sustainability, and Economy indicators across countries. The headline data product, the WISE Database, sits at [beyond-gdp.world/wise-database](https://beyond-gdp.world/wise-database/) (the data-products portal of WISE Horizons). The published claim is over one million data points, 244 metrics, 218 countries, and 61 country groupings, harmonised from international institutions and academic publications across the three WISE dimensions. Project home: [wisehorizons.world](https://wisehorizons.world/). The OECD's own WISE Centre at [oecd.org/wise](https://www.oecd.org/wise/) is the institutional analogue.

What this offers for our model. Three things. First, an alternative belonging-and-cohesion indicator with explicit cross-country comparability, which can be swapped in for or compared against the OECD How's Life support-network item. Second, an explicit Snower-Lima de Miranda dashboard of the same SAGE dimensions we are using, which means our country rows can be benchmarked against their published per-country dimension scores (an external check on the alpha and B gradients). Third, this is the audience: a model calibrated against the WISE Database, citing it openly, and offering structural counterfactuals their dashboards do not produce, is a direct fit for the programme's stated agenda.

A practical note. The WISE Database is in active development and the public download interface is partial as of writing; the project lists a contribute-and-request channel for finer-grained metric exports. For the paper, the right move is to cite the database as the harmonised cross-country source for the SAGE dimensions, name the OECD How's Life and World Values Survey items we actually use as the underlying inputs (which the database also draws on), and offer benchmarking against WISE's per-country dashboard scores as a robustness check the project would value.

## Route 4: the Stanford GDP-B programme (the methodology precedent)

GDP-B is the Brynjolfsson-Diewert-Eggers-Fox project at the [Stanford Digital Economy Lab](https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/research/gdp-b-a-new-well-being-metric-in-the-era-of-the-digital-economy/), originally for valuing free digital goods that GDP misses. The technical paper is [NBER w25695](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25695). The methodology: online incentive-compatible choice experiments at scale, asking people what they would pay to keep a good or service, to recover shadow prices for non-marketed objects. The 2026 GDP-B workshop at Stanford signals continued development.

Why this is relevant for the SAGE programme. GDP-B is the methodology precedent for what we would do to discipline Lambda from primary data. Their question is "what is the shadow price of Facebook"; our analogue is "what is the shadow price of a unit of associative time". The experiment is the same: present incentive-compatible choices that reveal willingness to substitute between social participation and consumption, run at scale, recover a population-representative shadow price. Replacing self-selected YBLI weights with an incentive-compatible choice-experiment estimate is exactly the upgrade the GDP-B programme has been validating, and the same audience reads both. Citing GDP-B in the methodology section of the Lambda estimation note positions the project alongside an active, well-funded measurement programme.

## What this means for the next iteration, in one paragraph

The current calibration uses YBLI for Lambda and is honest about the sample weakness. Three upgrade paths exist and three different audiences read them: the WELLBY literature (panel-data shadow prices, the UK Treasury wellbeing-appraisal audience), the WISE Database (Snower-Lima de Miranda's own programme, the wellbeing-policy audience and the natural collaboration target), and the Stanford GDP-B programme (incentive-compatible online choice experiments, the digital-measurement audience). The substantive implication of the WELLBY route is the largest in absolute terms: it would imply a wellbeing-side Lambda about two to three times higher, sharpening the decoupling result without changing its sign. The WISE Database is the most actionable for the immediate paper because it provides a harmonised cross-country indicator and is the project's natural intellectual home; the right cite-and-benchmark move is to use it as a robustness check on alpha and B. GDP-B is the methodological signpost for the upgrade beyond either: the way to replace any indirect weighting (whether YBLI's self-selected ratings or WELLBY's regression coefficients) with a direct elicitation that the measurement community already considers gold standard.

The papers and the engine will follow this hierarchy: YBLI as the documented baseline, WELLBY as the upgrade route already designed (Lambda Estimation Design note in the vault), WISE as the published-data robustness check, GDP-B as the methodological frontier for the eventual primary elicitation. Every layer of the hierarchy is cited so a careful reader (or referee, or potential collaborator) can see exactly where any value comes from and what its honest standing is.
